Problem 1: Minimum separation of the two peaks using Fourier analysis

For two infinitely narrow slits, the optical disturbance emerging from the mask can be represented by $$ M(x') = e^{-i\pi/2} \delta\left(x' + \frac{p}{2}\right) + e^{i\pi/2} \delta\left(x' - \frac{p}{2}\right). $$

  1. Find the Fourier transform of $M$ which is proportional to the optical disturbance in the Fourier plane (also called the back focal plane and the pupil plane).
    Solution:

    This is straightforward; we use the standard properties of $\delta$:

    $$ \begin{aligned} \mathcal{F}[M](k) &= e^{-i\pi/2}\int dx\, \, \delta\left(x + \frac{p}{2}\right) \, e^{-ikx} + e^{i\pi/2} \int dx \, \, \delta\left(x - \frac{p}{2}\right) \, e^{-ikx} \\ &= e^{-i\pi/2} \, e^{ikp/2} + e^{i\pi/2} e^{-ikp/2} \\ &= 2\cos\left((kp - \pi)/2\right) \\ &= 2\sin(kp/2). \end{aligned} $$
  2. As the separation of the two slits becomes $\ll 0.5\lambda/\operatorname{NA}$, approximate this function by a linear function that drops to zero outside the NA-defining aperture of the imaging system.
    Solution:

    Using $\sin \alpha \approx \alpha$ for $\alpha \ll 1$, $\mathcal{F}[M](k) \approx 2 \cdot kp/2 = kp$. To make sure that it vanishes outside the NA-defining aperture of the imaging system, we multiply to get $kp \cdot \operatorname{rect}(k\lambda/2 \operatorname{NA})$.

  3. Perform a Fourier transform on this function to find the optical disturbance in the wafer plane and show that the lower limit of the separation of the two peak intensity is $0.66 \lambda/\operatorname{NA}$, the same answer that we found last time by directly analyzing the impulse responses from these slits.
    Solution:

    We apply an inverse Fourier transform:

    $$ \begin{aligned} \mathcal{F}^{-1}[\mathcal{F}_{\operatorname{approx}}[M](k)](x) &= \int dk \, kp \cdot \operatorname{rect}(k\lambda/2 \operatorname{NA}) \, e^{ikx} \\ &= p \int_{-a}^{+a} dk\, k \, e^{ikx}, \end{aligned} $$

    where $a = 2\pi \operatorname{NA}/\lambda$. Evaluating this yields

    $$ \begin{aligned} E(x) = 2ip \left ( \frac{\sin(ax) - ax\cos(ax)}{x^2} \right), \end{aligned} $$

    so that

    $$ \begin{aligned} I(x) = 4p^2 \left ( \frac{\sin(ax) - ax\cos(ax)}{x^2} \right)^2, \end{aligned} $$

    which is symmetric. Solving numerically for its maximum yields $ax = 2\pi \operatorname{NA}/\lambda \cdot x = 2.08$, or $x = 0.33 \, \, \lambda/\operatorname{NA}$. The total distance between the peaks, due to $I$ being symmetric, is $0.66 \, \, \lambda/\operatorname{NA}$, in agreement with the prior homework.

Problem 2: Image intensity and contrast

Patterns of multiple periods (or pitches in lithographic lingo) may appear on the same photomask. If a mask containing lines and spaces of the pitch $p = 2k_1 \lambda/\operatorname{NA}$ where $0.25 < k_1 < 0.5$ is exposed with dipole illumination at optimal angles $\pm \sin^{-1}(\lambda/2p)$, then the light intensity is $I(x, z) = 2(A^2 + B^2 + 2AB \cos (2\pi x/p))$ and, in theory, the depth of focus (DOF) for these lines is infinitely large, as was shown in class.

Under the above illumination configuration (i.e., dipole illumination optimized for printing lines of pitch $p$), for lines and spaces of equal width but of pitch $2p$ (Fourier coefficients $c_0 = 1/2$, $c_{\pm 1} = 1/\pi$, $c_{\pm 2} = 0$):

  1. Derive an expression for the image intensity.
    Solution:

    To start, we consider just the first pole with angle $\sin \theta = \lambda/2p$. Thus, we have that

    $$ \begin{aligned} \sin \theta_m = \lambda/2p + m \lambda/(2p), \end{aligned} $$

    so that $\sin \theta_0 = \lambda/2p$, $\sin \theta_1 = \lambda/p$, and $\sin \theta_{-1} = 0$. From this, we can observe that $\sin \theta_1 = \lambda/p = \operatorname{NA}/2k_1 > \operatorname{NA}$, for $k_1 < 0.5$, implying that the $1$st order doesn't get through, while the others do. Hence, the electric field is given by

    $$ \begin{aligned} E(x) &= c_0 e^{i2\pi x \sin(\theta_0) /\lambda} + c_{-1} e^{i 2\pi x \sin(\theta_{-1})/\lambda} \\ &= 1/2 \, e^{i\pi x/p} + 1/\pi. \end{aligned} $$

    Then, the single-pole intensity is

    $$ \begin{aligned} I(x) = |E(x)|^2 &= 1/4 + 1/\pi^2 + 1/\pi \cdot (e^{i\pi x/p} + e^{-i\pi x/p})/2 \\ &= 1/4 + 1/\pi^2 + 1/\pi \cdot \cos(\pi x/p). \end{aligned} $$

    Now, we consider the second pole. We can observe that $E(x)$ from it will merely be the conjugate of the first pole's, and hence the intensity just doubles, yielding

    $$ \begin{aligned} I(x) = 1/2 + 2/\pi^2 + 2/\pi \cdot \cos(\pi x/p). \end{aligned} $$
  2. Derive an expression for the image contrast that is $z$-dependent.
    Solution:

    The $z$-dependent expression for $E_1(x)$ is

    $$ \begin{aligned} E_1(x, z) &= \frac{1}{2} e^{i \pi x/p} \, e^{i 2\pi z \sqrt{1 - (\lambda/2p)^2}/\lambda} + \frac{1}{\pi} e^{2i\pi z/\lambda}, \end{aligned} $$

    so that the intensity is given by

    $$ \begin{aligned} I_1(x, z) = |E_1(x, z)|^2 &= 1/4 + 1/\pi^2 + \frac{1}{\pi} \cos \left ( \frac{\pi x}{p} - \frac{2\pi z}{\lambda} \cdot \left(1 - \sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{\lambda}{2p}\right)^2} \right)\right). \end{aligned} $$

    Similarly, the intensity from the second pole is given by

    $$ \begin{aligned} I_2(x, z) = |E_2(x, z)|^2 &= 1/4 + 1/\pi^2 + \frac{1}{\pi} \cos \left ( \frac{\pi x}{p} + \frac{2\pi z}{\lambda} \cdot \left(1 - \sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{\lambda}{2p}\right)^2} \right)\right). \end{aligned} $$

    The total intensity is then given by, using the identity $\cos(\alpha + \beta) + \cos(\alpha - \beta) = 2\cos(\alpha) \cos(\beta)$,

    $$ \begin{aligned} I(x, z) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{2}{\pi^2} + \frac{2}{\pi} \cos(\pi x/p) \cos\left(\frac{2\pi z}{\lambda} \cdot \left ( 1 - \sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{\lambda}{2p}\right)^2}\right)\right). \end{aligned} $$

    Hence, the contrast is

    $$ \begin{aligned} C = \frac{I_{\max} - I_{\min}}{I_{\max} + I_{\min}} = \frac{4/\pi }{1 + 4/\pi^2} \cos\left(\frac{2\pi z}{\lambda} \cdot \left ( 1 - \sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{\lambda}{2p}\right)^2}\right)\right). \end{aligned} $$
  3. Assuming a minimum contrast of approximately $0.7$ at the edge of defocus, write an expression for the DOF in units of $k_1^2 \lambda/\operatorname{NA}^2$. Assuming $\lambda = 13.5 \operatorname{nm}$, $\operatorname{NA} = 0.55$, and $k_1 = 0.4$ what is the approximate DOF (in $\operatorname{nm}$)?
    Solution:

    Substituting the value of $p = 2k_1 \lambda/\operatorname{NA}$, we obtain that

    $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{2\pi z}{\lambda} \cdot \left ( 1 - \sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{\operatorname{NA}}{4k_1}\right)^2}\right) &= \cos^{-1}\left( 0.7 \cdot \frac{1 + 4/\pi^2}{4/\pi }\right) = 0.69 \\ &= \frac{\pi z \operatorname{NA}^2}{16k_1^2 \lambda}, \end{aligned} $$

    where we used the binomial approximation. From this, we can observe that

    $$ \begin{aligned} \operatorname{DoF} = 2z = 2 \cdot 0.69 \cdot \frac{16}{\pi} \frac{k_1^2 \lambda}{\operatorname{NA}^2} \approx 7.02 \, \frac{k_1^2 \lambda}{\operatorname{NA}^2}. \end{aligned} $$

    Substituting and solving for $z$ yields $z = 25 \operatorname{nm}$, or $\operatorname{DoF} = 50 \operatorname{nm}$.
